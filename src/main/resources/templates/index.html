Intro

The overall purpose of this project was to gain a better understanding of the different stakeholders’ perspectives on environmental policy and the overall challenges facing the Chesapeake Bay and the subsequent management and policies moving forward.

When considering the state of the Chesapeake Bay and its overall health over time, it is important to consider the multiple effects, influences, ideas, and perspectives that different stakeholders within the region may have towards the Bay. Although there has been progress in restoring and protecting the Bay, there is still evidence of man-induced problems such as pollution and overharvesting. In addition to the environmental issues facing the Bay region, there are many different ideas and proposed solutions to amend the problems.

Adding to the conundrum of what should be done to restore the Bay, political divisions slow down the process and hinder results towards a cleaner and safer Bay. There are also deep divisions among the different stakeholders about the imbalance between putting an influence on protecting the Bay or protecting the economic livelihood of the different stakeholders in the region. Farmers may contribute nitrogen, phosphorus, and sediment pollution by what actions they take on their land. Watermen may overharvest key species such as oysters that help contribute to the health of the Bay. In contrast, as scientists provide and collect data, policy advocates may push policy makers to enforce more regulations, which can often hurt different stakeholders in the process. Hopefully, hearing and incorporating the views and perspectives of all stakeholders will increase the chance of restoration and survival of our Chesapeake Bay.

Background Information

The Chesapeake Bay is the largest estuary in North America. With a watershed spanning 64,000 miles from Norfolk, VA to Cooperstown, NY, across six states, the bay faces many challenges both political and environmental.

The bay was once a thriving environment for not only the oysters, crabs and many other species living in its waters but also for the watermen who harvest its resources. Once harvesting tens of millions bushels of oysters now down to about 1% of that, and providing 500 million pounds of seafood annually to the United States.

In recent years the health of the bay has been an ongoing debate between the people deemed experts like policy makers or scholars and the people who work the land/water like farmers or watermen. With declining oyster and crab populations, everyone can agree that there is something wrong with the bay and if progress is not made, this national treasure may be loss forever.

There have been policies put in place in order to regulate Bay health, although they each have varying degrees of effectiveness. One of the biggest pieces of legislation would be the 2009 Chesapeake Bay Executive Order that was signed by President Obama where the Bay was given the label of a national treasure. In doing this, the government at all levels were incentivized to renew and restore Bay health. This was carried out by an appointed committee, which was tasked with: create innovating solutions to Bay health, target resources to better protect the tributaries of the Bay, create a better plan for protecting against excess sediment and nutrients brought in by storm waters, and expand environmental research such as climate change.

There are also other policies that work in tangent with the executive order, such as the Clean Water Act. First introduced in 1948 as the Federal Water Pollution and Control Act, the legislation had seen a couple of revisions, which eventually became the Water Quality Control Act of 1977. The main goal is to ensure the quality of the water was both “swimmabe and fishable.” This is done through stricter enforcements, such as heavy fines for pollution, restrictions on dredging, and state compliance to meet the water quality standard set forth by TMDL’s (Total Maximum Daily Loads), which are the maximum amount of nutrients and pollutants each state/district in the watershed is allowed to let run off into the Bay and its rivers.

Scientist/Policy Advocate

What are your biggest concerns when it comes to saving the Bay? How is it affecting your daily life?

My biggest concern is that saving the bay is a generational commitment which means you can’t just appropriate money for a few years or stay strong on a policy for one election cycle. It will take sustained commitment not just to meet the clean-up goals by 2025, but to stay below the pollution cap once it is reached forever. That means every bit of population growth, development or infrastructure needs to offset any increases in nitrogen and phosphorus pollution, as well as begin to reverse trends in toxic contamination. The way this reality effects my daily life is in the way I advocate for pollution control policies for CBF. I can never look at the remarkable progress made in pollution reduction in the bay since 2010 and be satisfied that it is good enough because those reductions came from hard-fought policies put into place as much as a decade before. The science I’m evaluating and the policies CBF is advocating are what will be needed to stay below the pollution cap in 2025 and beyond. I’m essentially creating a legacy.

When considering new legislation, what factors do you consider or believe should be considered before pushing forward?

First of all, don’t fix what ain’t broke. Many advances in pollution reduction, habitat restoration or species recovery can be accomplished through good governance and existing laws. That means we can use the pressure of our comment letters, constituent engagement with members and their elected officials and judicious use of the press to push for funding and enforcement support of our existing laws. If the agencies responsible for such governance are recalcitrant, then we may sponsor legislation that “clarifies” the role of the agency. Often just the threat to do so brings the agency in line because they want to maintain control over their mandates and resources. Much of our consideration of legislation is to defend against bad bills brought by special interests that enrich their businesses at the expense of the bay. In any given legislative session, that is probably two thirds of our work. Passing legislation is very difficult, so we also work with other environmental groups to decide on a few priority bills that we will work on together. This is hard because the bills chosen by the group may not necessarily be a priority for CBF, but we will appreciate their help when it’s our turn.

When does scientific data related to Bay health and environmental protection take precedence over people's economic livelihood when making or advocating for policy decisions?

It’s an important ingredient, but I don’t fool myself into thinking that the scientific argument wins the battle. My job is to paint a picture given the scientific realities of the implementation of an existing or proposed policy in context with how the bay works. For example, a fairly new policy that targets phosphorus saturation in agricultural soils over-fertilized by chicken manure is being phased in over a 5 year period. The phosphorus has been washing into the bay for decades and will get worse if this policy isn’t put into place. One of my staff is an agricultural economist. We consider the economic impact of this new policy and agreed to the 5-year phase in as a strategy to address the cost for farmers to switch practices as well as allow time for new technologies of manure treatment and transport to come online. The industry continues to call for a delay in the implementation, so I will continue to evaluate the risk of continued phosphorus delivery to the clean up effort and continue to make my case. This example is nuanced and the way it works most of the time. Occasionally, the scientific data describe a smoking gun, where an immediate change in policy is necessary. I think we are there with climate change and rather than considering new policies as an economic burden, I consider them an opportunity to shift the economy in a different direction. There will always be winners and losers, but there are reasons why you don’t see blacksmiths and typewriter repairmen any more.

At this point, do you believe the 2025 targets are achievable for the TMDL, and in what way do you think we could improve reaching the 2025 target?

They are achievable in Maryland based on the seriousness this state made to the clean-up effort years ago and the flexibility we have built in to our policies and funding mechanisms. Virginia is close too, but Pennsylvania is far behind in reaching the targets. Thankfully, the bay is responding well to the pollution reductions we’ve already achieved and most of the bay will likely meet water quality standards by 2025 or shortly thereafter even given the slower watershed-wide progress. To improve reaching the target, all jurisdictions need to employ targeting tools like Geographic Information Systems and our knowledge of the cost-effectiveness of certain practices like tree planting in riparian buffers to optimize the dollars spent. We also need to develop robust nutrient trading programs that will allow a system of offsets for new growth as mentioned in the first question.

What challenges do you believe hinder the results of Bay restoration efforts the most?

Our climate is already highly destabilized. The torrential rains we had in 2018 delivered several years’ worth of nitrogen and phosphorus loads to the bay, which may set back our progress, just as seagrasses have reached a 30 year high and the dead zone had been the smallest in a decade. The full clean-up must be accomplished to give the bay a measure of resiliency to these kinds of events. Urban stormwater loads continue to go up despite millions of dollars being spent on urban runoff practices. Nutrient trading could help, but society needs to get real with how much individual space we need to live as the population of the region continues to grow.

Are there any policies, regulations, or changes you see to be the most necessary for Chesapeake Bay cleanup?

All 3 states need to do a better of job of forest retention and replanting, whether in agricultural landscapes or urban areas. Nothing reduces runoff and nutrient loading throughout the watershed better than mature forests. Second to that, we need to rebuild the oyster reef ecosystems in the bay. In addition to water filtration, reefs calm nearshore waters reducing the resuspension of fine sediments. Both of these ecosystem processes increase the available area for bay grasses and shoreline marshes that will address sea level rise. The increased amount of oyster shell can help buffer the bay’s waters against ocean acidification and reefs support up to 300 other species in the bay’s food web.

What is your understanding of the progress of the bay? Do you think it is improving, declining or staying the same?

This is where the science has been most clear and personally exciting for me. The bay clean-up progress is tracked through the Chesapeake Bay Model, which tracks changes in land use and the implementation of best management practices to simulate load reductions in nitrogen and phosphorus. According to the model, even with population growth, we have reduced the nitrogen load by 10s of millions of pounds since 1983, the year I graduated from High School. In the complex chemistry of how nutrients drive phytoplankton blooms and dissolved oxygen, the load reductions must reach a certain threshold before the bay’s ecology changes. We are beginning to see certain water quality and living resources thresholds become visible in the bay, if even for brief periods of the year. This is our indicator that the bay clean up is working and that going through the process to get to 2025 loads should actually allow us to meet water quality standards for the first time in my life. So, had you asked me that question when I first started working at CBF in 2013, I would have been forced to say that the bay had stayed the same for 30 years despite all of the nutrient load reduction efforts. But in the last 3 years, everything has changed and I have a lot of hope for the future if we continue the hard work we started.

Farmer

What are your biggest concerns when it comes to saving the bay?

The bay is important to us for many reasons it’s a food source, a shipping source, and just an awesome place to visit and see nature.

How is it affecting your daily life?

Since our farm is on a tributary that feeds the Chesapeake we are strictly watched to not allow animals to wade in the water or drink out of the water.

Do you know which tributary? Or what body of water your farm directly flows into?

Antietam that flows into the Potomac

Have any recent policies negatively affected the way you run your farm or the amount you are able to produce?

We had to fence off a natural source of water because of the animals getting access to the river. That obviously has caused a higher use of our water pumps to supply water to them.

So, environmental best management practices, such as stream side fencing have had negative effects on your family’s overall production or costs?

It has made for higher cost which in farming when you’re fighting with pennies as it is any cost increase is tough

Are there any measurements you believe work best to both help the bay but also not dig deep into your family’s pockets?

If there was some access to the water that lead to our property yet kept the animals out of the direct water way would help I guess, something we could utilize without the burden of additional costs.

What challenges do you believe hinder the results of Bay restoration efforts the most?

I strongly believe that it is more destroyed by our everyday citizens who over mow their lawns, then spread pesticides and fertilizers weekly on their lawns, flowers, etc. Also, the over building of homes on natural land. The land can no longer absorb the natural rain water because of too many homes.

Are there any policies, regulations, or changes you see to be the most necessary for Chesapeake Bay cleanup?

Need to control over population and housing growth on farm land along with growth in our forest. Prime example is the floods in Ellicott City...if you look at what was natural land its now covered in houses which caused basically a canal effect right into the city.

So, you see urban development as one of the bigger contributors to problems for the bay?

Yes, and really to be clearer it’s the over development of the lands not just development in general

What is your understanding of the progress of the bay? Do you think it is improving, declining or staying the same?

My understanding is it is improving.

Why do you think that? What sources do you use?

Recent election adds focused on some major changes in and around the bay. I also know that waterways near me have looked clearer than they seemed to be 10-15 years ago.

Which policy do you think should be given priority when concerning the bay region, environmental protection or economic growth?

Controlling urban sprawl and over development of lands...there is plenty of older buildings or houses that sit vacant that could be used......also need to focus on the invasive fish like the catfish that humans placed there that are destroying our oyster bays

Scholar

What are your biggest concerns when it comes to saving the bay? How is it affecting your daily life, if any?

From a personal perspective, the pollution in the bay makes it a wasted opportunity for recreational activities and will only worsen if something is not done. I also enjoy diversity in the animal and plant life within the region and would be disappointed should that be reduced. Unfortunately, I have little experience with the bay as a whole and local opinion makes me less enthusiastic to utilize it. As such, my daily life is mostly unaffected.

When considering new legislation, what factors do you consider or believe should be considered before pushing forward?

First and foremost, the health of the population surrounding the bay should be prioritized. Policies also need to consider the long-term effects and be based on solid scientific data. Policies regarding economic concerns should consider how any change could influence the region as a whole. Higher pollution could reduce property value and lower recreational-based businesses but too many restrictions could also limit economic growth and increase the prices of certain products (e.g. agricultural products).

When does scientific data related to Bay health and environmental protection take precedence over people's economic livelihood when making or advocating for policy decisions?

Similar to the responses above, scientific data related to the health of the population surrounding the bay should take the highest precedence. Likewise, any evidence that would indicate that pollution is approaching a threshold of nearly irreversible damage to any of the associated ecosystems should also be considered very strongly. Additionally, if the scientific data suggests that reasonable changes to current activities could improve their effect on the bay (e.g. alternative farming practices or the identification of critical contaminants) then the legislation should reflect that.

What challenges do you believe hinder the results of Bay restoration efforts the most?

Some of the major issues that I feel hinder the restoration efforts are: 1) the size of the population the utilizes the Chesapeake Bay and its watershed. 2) The number of different states that are connected to the watershed.

The Chesapeake Bay itself is predominantly in MD but the greater watershed is spread to neighboring states. Notably, agricultural heavy states like PA likely contribute a lot to the pollution but convincing the state to pass legislation that would limit their own populace would be a hard sell. As such, it is likely hard to form proper legislation when it relies on cooperation from states displaced from the actual bay or action from the federal government.

Are there any policies, regulations, or changes you see to be the most necessary for Chesapeake Bay cleanup?

  1. Increasing the education of how actions influence the bay health
  2. Emphasizing to the public why this is important in their lives.
  3. Limitations should be placed regarding the development within the Chesapeake Bay watershed.
  4. Increase the publicity of current policies and initiatives.

What is your understanding of the progress of the bay? Do you think it is improving, declining or staying the same?

I have heard that there are goals to improve the quality of the Baltimore Inner Harbor, which I assume is also a goal for the neighboring regions as well. The fact that it is being discussed would suggest to me that there is an active effort underway, but the specifics of how this is to be done have not been as apparent. I have heard nothing about the policies from adjacent states. Overall, I would say that the quality could be increasing but there is much room for improvement.

Since you are a Vermont native, have you noticed any difference in the way Maryland handles environmental policy vs. how Vermont handles environmental concerns?

Oddly enough the city of Burlington, the only metropolitan-like area in VT, still dumps a lot of waste into the Lake Champlain and there are a lot of concerns with the sanitation issues that result from this. Overall, though, the area seems cleaner and several differences likely contribute to that. 1) The population and population densities are much lower (the state’s population is nearly identical to Baltimore City proper)– so the shear amount of waste is lower. 2) There is a lot of discussion at the public level with regards to being green and the city itself is completely powered by renewable energy. So, there is more knowledge of the concerns. 3) The population is aging and many of the residents are retirees that moved there for the reason to keep it green.

As for initiatives in VT, there has been legislation put in place to lower the phosphorus levels that drain from agricultural work and a group even sued the EPA to lower the maximum allowed phosphorus run-off. With regards to other environmental concerns, VT has building restrictions to prevent changing much of the natural feel, but this may also be more doable as a result of a smaller population. There is also a law requiring mandatory composting for restaurants and individuals by 2020. In contrast, the Chesapeake Bay area includes a much higher population and includes a very large watershed and the initiatives are less apparent to me.
